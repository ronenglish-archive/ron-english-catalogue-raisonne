Thought for a few seconds For a Ron English catalogue raisonné, a bibliography would be the section that documents the published sources connected to his work, career, exhibitions, and reception. It gives scholars, collectors, curators, and galleries a way to trace where information came from and where to keep researching. In practice, it would usually include several kinds of material. First, books and monographs. That means artist books, exhibition catalogues, survey books, museum publications, books on pop surrealism or contemporary art that discuss Ron, and any books written by Ron or featuring substantial reproductions of his work. Second, exhibition publications. This would cover solo and group exhibition catalogues, gallery brochures, checklists, fair publications, and museum handouts when they contain useful documentation. Third, periodicals and press. That includes magazine features, newspaper articles, art journal reviews, interviews, and significant online articles. For Ron, this could also include coverage of POPaganda, billboard interventions, street works, collaborations, and major public projects. Fourth, reference and archival sources. These are things like gallery press releases, museum collection pages, archived webpages, auction catalogues, festival programs, and other primary-source records that help verify dates, titles, dimensions, materials, locations, or exhibition history. Fifth, audiovisual sources, if you want the bibliography to be more complete. That could include documentaries, recorded interviews, lectures, podcasts, and video coverage of exhibitions or installations. For your project, the bibliography could serve two slightly different purposes. One option is a scholarly bibliography. This is fuller and more formal. It aims to document every substantial source used across the catalogue raisonné. The other is a practical research bibliography. This is more useful for a web project in progress. It focuses on the sources you actually relied on to build entries, verify works, and trace exhibitions. A strong bibliography entry usually includes the author, title, publication or publisher, date, page numbers if relevant, and sometimes a note about what it documents. For web sources, you would also want the site name, publication date if available, and access date if the material is likely to change. For a catalogue raisonné website, I would usually suggest splitting it into sections such as: Primary sources Exhibition catalogues Books Articles and interviews Auction and sales records Online archives and institutional sources Audiovisual sources You could also decide whether it lives in one master bibliography page or in two layers: a master bibliography for the whole site, plus shorter source notes on individual artwork or exhibition pages. For Ron English specifically, the bibliography would help with things like confirming when a work appeared publicly, tracking alternate titles, documenting collaborations, establishing exhibition timelines, and separating firsthand documentation from later repetition. If you want this to feel polished and useful, I would recommend these standards: Use one citation style across the whole site. Separate primary sources from secondary sources. Mark sources you have personally verified from scans, catalogues, or archival copies. Avoid listing weak sources unless they provide unique evidence. Keep exhibition pages tied to the bibliography when possible. So the short version is: it is not just a reading list. It is the research backbone of the catalogue raisonné.
